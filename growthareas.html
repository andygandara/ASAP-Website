Advanced Armour

Produce a lightweight vest for soldiers and police officers that can stop an armor-piercing bullet.

Expect the defense companies to take on this challenge. Dupont already markets its Kevlar products, and Ceradyne manufactures ceramic armor plates. Lockheed Martin announced in January that it will sell armor jackets that can power soldiers' electronic devices.

Expect big advances in this space from Iceland's Ossur.

Air Traffic Control

Move to a more accurate satellite-based surveillance system.

In September, Washington began using satellite radar (as opposed to traditional, ground-based radar systems) to track commercial aircraft in the Gulf of Mexico. Satellite activity by telecoms such as AT&T and Iridium could cut technological costs, which would make launching and maintaining satellite systems more efficient. A system could roll out nationwide by 2013.

Battery Technology for Electric Vehicles

Produce batteries and components to power affordable electric cars.

The Administration has pledged $2.4 billion to support battery innovation, galvanizing an industry that's looking to develop batteries that enable cars to drive hundreds of miles on a single charge. GE, IBM, and Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway are getting in on the game, taking stakes in companies such as Chinese automaker BYD and Watertown (Mass.)-based A123 System to develop next-generation lithium ion, lithium oxide, and sodium batteries.

Green Biofuels

Produce biofuels to displace oil consumption and reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Washington has declared that by 2010, America's annual fuel consumption must include 100 million gallons of advanced biofuels. By 2022, 21 billion gallons of fuel must come from sources other than food crops. Archer Daniels Midland has partnered with Deere and Monsanto to turn cornfield waste (the parts of the plants not eaten) into fuel. With support from GM, Warrenville (Ill.)-based renewable energy firm Coskata is working on a system to break down cellulosic biomass—as well as certain kinds of trash—and convert it to biofuel.

Cell-Targeting Medicine

Develop smart, anti-cancer therapeutics.

Nanotechnology and microtechnology researchers are refining medicines that can deliver drugs only to disease-infected cells. Most of the advanced work is happening in academic centers such as the University of Chicago Medical Center and the Energy Dept.'s Argonne National Laboratory. Bet on the pharmaceutical industry to test and bring the new treatments to market.

Cognitive Radio

Develop phones and wireless devices that can switch between frequencies within the radio spectrum.

Dell, Google, and Hewlett-Packard have petitioned the Federal Communications Commission to make unused TV spectrum available for cognitive devices, which can seamlessly switch between radio frequencies whether using the Internet, TV, or a cellular signal. A network with cognitive functionality could continually adapt and allocate resources according to customer needs and demands.

Disposal of Toxic/Radioactive Waste

Dispose of radioactive wastes and toxic chemicals quickly, inexpensively, and safely.

While Walmart may not develop the actual technology, its pressure on global suppliers to focus on product sustainability is already forcing innovations upon businesses that want their products stocked on the retail giant's shelves. Meanwhile, companies such as Dell and Apple are engineering electronics that don't create waste in the first place. Other advances will come from government agencies such as the Environmental Protection Agency when further iterations of nuclear power are explored.

DNA Sequencing

Analyze and map all forms of cancer.

Wisconsin-based Third Wave Technologies earned approval from the Food & Drug Administration for the first DNA test for two types of human papilloma virus (HPV) that can lead to cervical cancer. Companies such as San Diego's Illumina and Foster City (Calif.)-based Navigenics currently offer more general, overall DNA sequencing. That technology will likely become more sophisticated over time, making cancer screening cheaper and faster.

Early Disease Diagnosis

Attain early detection of dozens of diseases via a saliva sample.

Bristol-Myers Squibb and Quest Diagnostics are already using spit samples to test for mutations that make some patients immune to certain treatments. Google-backed startup 23andMe collects saliva samples and looks for genetic variations in DNA that might reveal possible future diseases

High-Speed Rail

Build a high-speed rail network of 100- to 600-mile intercity corridors as one element of a modernized transportation system.

The Obama Administration has pledged $13 billion for high-speed train travel between cities such as New York and Washington. Some 24 states are vying for chunks of the $8 billion to be divvied up this winter. Several U.S. companies stand to benefit, including CSX, Burlington Northern Santa Fe, and Harsco.

Real-Time Language Translation

Create highly accurate, real-time translation between the major languages of the world.

On the edge are companies such as Waltham (Mass.)-based Lionbridge Technologies, which use human-assisted machine translation. A computer program tackles the first round of word-and-sentence conversion, about 90% of the work. A human then corrects mistakes, clarifies sentences, and refines the language. It's not instant yet—the industry averages rates of 5,000 words a day at around 12¢ to 15¢ a word—but progress in computing power and language research could one day make real-time translation a reality.

Massive Storage Density Memory

Fit the contents of the Library of Congress on a device the size of a sugar cube.

AT&T and other telecommunications companies already use sophisticated data-compression technologies to store vast sums of information and will likely push to attain Obama's dream. Internet companies such as Facebook and Myspace, which already hold information on millions of users, say that flash storage is crucial to their futures. That has companies such as EMC, IBM, and HP working to squeeze more memory onto flash drives.

Content-Rich Mobile Libraries

Build a rich, interactive, digital library for every child.

Amazon's Kindle e-reader ignited an explosion in mobile book and magazine reading. Google Books' collection of digitized literature is growing. Apple is rumored to be working on a tablet device that would work with iTunes to create an accessible, mobile library.

Regenerative Medicine - Organ Growth

Develop regenerative medicine that can create an organ or regrow a limb.

Amgen says its prowess in cellular and molecular biology places the company in a prime position to uncover treatments to repair or replace tissue and organs. The Wake Forest Institute for Regenerative Medicine in Winston-Salem, N.C. was the first outfit to grow an organ in a lab, implying that it might have success with limb regrowth. But much of the progress will be determined by progress in the politically charged area of stem-cell research.

Personalized Prescriptions

Produce personalized medicine that enables an instant prescription of the right dose of the right drug. Mostly still a topic confined to academic research, the likely businesses to cash in will be pharmaceutical companies working in conjunction with DNA-sequencing outfits. Local pharmacies also see personalized prescriptions as an opportunity, given their direct, personal access to patients.

Self-Sufficient Buildings

Construct green buildings that produce all the energy they consume.

Look for companies such as United Technologies and Honeywell to develop industrial-specification, energy-efficient systems for heating, ventilation, and water. Some of the $2.8 billion in Obama's stimulus plan earmarked for residential green investments could help revive the battered housing and manufacturing markets, too.

Efficient Cost Effective Solar Cells

Create solar cells that cost as little as paint.

Dow Chemical recently announced solar roof shingles that use low-cost, thin-film photovoltaic cells and don't require special installation skills. They'll be available by 2011. Ascent Solar Technologies is set to begin shipping highly efficient solar cells made from copper, indium, gallium, and selenium early next year.

Advanced Super Computers

Obama's Challenge: Build an "exascale" supercomputer capable of a million trillion calculations per second.

Who's Got a Shot: IBM's $133 million Roadrunner is currently the world's fastest computer—the first to make a thousand trillion calculations per second. Labs run by HP, Microsoft, and DARPA are also refining technology to hit Obama's goal, which will likely be achieved within a decade.

Immersive Teaching Software

Build educational software that's as compelling as the best video game—and as effective as a personal tutor.

Chicago-based PrepMe has developed customizable test-prep software that can track a student's progress and tailor the necessary educational experience. Now the company plans to expand its offerings into software for the classroom. Rosetta Stone's innovations in language-teaching software—including the addition of live chats with experts and the introduction of game-like, logic-based learning exercises—could be expanded to include many subjects.

Vaccines for Every Flu Strain

Develop a universal vaccine for influenza that will protect against all future strains.

With hundreds of strains circulating of an ever-morphing virus, a universal vaccine isn't likely soon. But the closer a vaccine can match a strain, the less severe the virus's effects will be. The major U.S. players in this space are Baxter, VaxInnate, Pfizer, Novavax, and Merck.
